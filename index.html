Playing a Fantasy-Based RPG to Understand Other Cultures and Thoughts to Grow Personally
Are you interested in learning about other cultures and perspectives? Consider playing a fantasy-based role-playing game where you can immerse yourself in a new world and explore the beliefs and values of different characters.
In this game, you could play as a wizard who leads a peaceful burg, but the world is filled with ancient hidden magic and monsters. As the world begins to use magic in industrialized ways to reduce suffering, resources become limited and new challenges arise.
To get started, try these prompts:
  • Choose a character from a culture different from your own. Research and learn about that culture's traditions, beliefs, and values to create a more authentic character.
  • Explore how your character's unique perspective and experiences can influence their interactions with other characters and the world around them.
  • Collaborate with other players to solve problems and overcome obstacles in the game, using your character's strengths and weaknesses to contribute to the group's success.
  • Take on challenges that test your character's beliefs and values, and explore how they grow and change as a result.
  • Reflect on your own personal growth and learning throughout the game, and consider how it may apply to real-world situations and interactions with others.
  • Embrace the idea that the game is about the journey, not the destination. Don't focus on "winning," but instead on the experience of learning and growing through play.
  • Keep an open mind and be willing to learn from others' perspectives and experiences, both in the game and in real life.
  • Have fun and enjoy the process! Remember that learning and personal growth can be exciting and rewarding, and that games can be a great way to engage with new ideas and experiences.
Exploring the Three Pillars of Roleplaying
Roleplaying is built on three pillars: combat, exploration, and social interaction. In a good roleplaying game, all three of these pillars are given equal weight and importance. Let's explore each one in turn with some example story starters:

Combat

Combat is often the most familiar and straightforward pillar of roleplaying. It involves battles, fights, and other violent conflicts. Here are some story starters that focus on combat:
  • You are part of a band of adventurers hired to clear out a dungeon that's been taken over by goblins. As you make your way deeper into the dungeon, you encounter increasingly fierce resistance from the goblin defenders.
  • You are a member of a tribe of barbarians that has been attacked by a neighboring tribe. The two sides have agreed to settle their differences with a ritual combat between champions. You are chosen to represent your tribe.
  • You are a knight sworn to protect a castle from a besieging army. As you ride out to meet the enemy, you notice that they have a powerful wizard leading them. You'll have to use all of your skill and bravery to defeat them.

Exploration

Exploration is about discovering new places, uncovering hidden secrets, and overcoming obstacles. Here are some story starters that focus on exploration:
  • You are a member of an expedition sent to map an uncharted jungle. As you make your way through the dense vegetation, you encounter strange creatures, ancient ruins, and treacherous terrain.
  • You are a sailor on a merchant ship that's been blown off course by a storm. You find yourself on a deserted island, with only your wits and your survival skills to keep you alive.
  • You are a spy sent on a mission to infiltrate a fortress. You must sneak past guards, disable traps, and find the information you were sent to retrieve.

Social Interaction

Social interaction is all about relationships, politics, and negotiation. It's about using your words and your charisma to achieve your goals. Here are some story starters that focus on social interaction:
  • You are a diplomat sent to negotiate a treaty between two warring nations. You must navigate complex cultural differences, hidden agendas, and personal animosities to find a way to bring peace.
  • You are a thief who has just joined a powerful thieves' guild. To rise through the ranks, you must prove your worth by completing dangerous missions and earning the trust of your fellow thieves.
  • You are a bard trying to make a name for yourself in the big city. You'll have to use your wit, charm, and musical talents to win over audiences, make contacts, and get ahead in the competitive world of entertainment.

The Basics of DND5 Combat with a Twist

Picture this, brave adventurers, a world of magic, danger, and excitement. You find yourself in the heat of battle, and the clock is ticking. Every move you make counts. You have one minute to play your action card or write down your action. Time is precious, and the stakes are high. Will you succeed?

Remember, everyone plays their action card or writes down their action in secret. The DM may pause the timer, but try to keep the adrenaline pumping. Once the timer is up, or everyone has finished, roll all the dice you need for your action. Take note of what rolls you would use in different conditions. This is where the real fun begins.

Everyone rolls dice to hit, deal damage, and make three ordered saving throws to be used in the order of action. Everything happens at the same time, all players and the DM. Then, evaluate everything in the order of initiative, and it can't be changed. Reactions must be played before the next action is evaluated. Tap twice on the table to show you're ready for the next round.

But remember, if you don't make it in time, no action. So, be brave, be bold, and make every move count!

Ability Checks in D&D 5E

What are Ability Checks?

Ability checks are a way to determine whether a character succeeds or fails at a task that requires some level of skill or luck. In D&D 5E, there are six ability scores that are used to determine the success of these checks:

  • Strength
  • Dexterity
  • Constitution
  • Intelligence
  • Wisdom
  • Charisma

When a character wants to attempt an action that is not covered by a spell or weapon attack, the DM will ask for an ability check. The player will then roll a 20-sided die (also known as a d20) and add the appropriate modifier for their ability score to the roll.

How to Calculate Ability Checks

To calculate an ability check, follow these steps:

  1. Determine which ability score is being used for the check.
  2. Roll a d20.
  3. Add the appropriate modifier for the ability score to the roll.

The result is the total for the ability check. If the result is equal to or greater than the DC (difficulty class) set by the DM, the character succeeds at the task. If the result is lower than the DC, the character fails.

The DC is determined by the DM based on the difficulty of the task. Easy tasks might have a DC of 10, while very hard tasks might have a DC of 20 or higher.

Examples of Ability Checks

Here are some examples of ability checks and which ability scores would be used for each:

  • Climbing a wall: Strength (Athletics)
  • Sneaking past guards: Dexterity (Stealth)
  • Recalling a piece of lore: Intelligence (History)
  • Sensing danger: Wisdom (Perception)
  • Persuading someone: Charisma (Persuasion)
Difficulty Classes
In D&D 5e, Difficulty Classes (DC) are used to determine the difficulty of an action that a player wants to take. A DC represents the minimum score that the player must achieve on a d20 roll plus their relevant modifier in order to succeed at the task. The following are the key ranges of difficulty and examples of actions that might correspond to them:
  • Very Easy (DC 5): Tasks that almost anyone could accomplish, such as opening an unlocked door or lifting a light object.
  • Easy (DC 10): Tasks that an average person could accomplish, such as finding a hidden object or persuading a friendly NPC to do something simple.
  • Moderate (DC 15): Tasks that require some skill or effort to accomplish, such as picking a lock or jumping across a small gap.
  • Hard (DC 20): Tasks that are difficult and require significant skill, such as climbing a sheer surface or persuading a hostile NPC to do something against their nature.
  • Very Hard (DC 25): Tasks that are almost impossible and require a great deal of skill or luck, such as finding a hidden object in a well-protected location or convincing an archmage to reveal their most guarded secrets.
  • Nearly Impossible (DC 30): Tasks that are virtually impossible and require both immense skill and incredible luck, such as sneaking past an entire army undetected or convincing a deity to intervene in mortal affairs.

Examples of When to Use Ability Checks

Athletics:

  • Jumping across a pit.
  • Climbing a wall.
  • Pushing a heavy object.

Acrobatics:

  • Performing a backflip to avoid an attack.
  • Tumbling through a narrow space.
  • Balancing on a tightrope.

Sleight of Hand:

  • Picking a lock.
  • Pickpocketing a target.
  • Planting evidence on someone.

Arcana:

  • Identifying a magical object.
  • Recalling knowledge about a specific spell.
  • Disabling a magical trap.

History:

  • Recalling knowledge about a historical event.
  • Researching information about a specific culture or society.
  • Identifying a historical artifact.

Insight:

  • Determining if someone is lying.
  • Reading a person's emotions or intentions.
  • Identifying a disguised or shapeshifted creature.

Perception:

  • Spotting a hidden creature or object.
  • Noticing a trap or ambush.
  • Hearing a faint noise or conversation.

Survival:

  • Tracking a creature through the wilderness.
  • Finding food and water in a harsh environment.
  • Building a shelter in the wilderness.

Intimidation:

  • Forcing a target to back down from a conflict.
  • Instilling fear in a target.
  • Convincing a target to do something against their will.

Persuasion:

  • Convince a hostile NPC to reveal information or not attack you
  • Negotiate a better price for goods or services with a merchant
  • Convince a skeptical ally to trust your plan or course of action
